01 —

Vision & Pillars

Bird Simulator is a controller-first, third-person roguelike about the complete life cycle of a bird — from hatchling to fledged nest — played in approximately 20 minutes. Every run is a single life. Permadeath is absolute and unkind. The tone is bright, chaotic, and beautiful: think Dark Souls difficulty wrapped in Katamari Damacy joy.

The game's central insight is that bird life is genuinely dangerous and genuinely gorgeous, and most games have never tried to capture both at once. A sparrow's world is full of sudden death and sudden wonder. We want players to feel both in the same breath.

Design Pillars

Awareness over reaction. The most powerful move in the game is stillness. Players who learn to read the environment before acting survive longer than players who are fast.

Economy of energy. Every action costs stamina. Every moment of rest is also a moment of vulnerability. There is no safe option.

Ecological fidelity. Bird classes, species, biomes, predators, and seasonal rhythms are grounded in real ornithology. The game should feel like a field guide that got too exciting.

Two buttons, infinite depth. The entire game is played with two contextual buttons plus movement. Mastery comes from reading context, not memorizing combos.

Every run is a gift. Unlocks are milestone-gated, not run-gated. The only thing that carries over is skill. A new player's tenth run should feel meaningfully better than their first.

02 —

Core Loop

Every player begins as an American Robin. Not a choice — a beginning. The robin is the right start: suburban, globally recognizable, generalist enough to teach every mechanic, aware enough to model the game's core philosophy. Jon Young's What the Robin Knows is an explicit touchstone — the robin's alarm calls as environmental intelligence, the bird as teacher of awareness. All other species and classes unlock through milestones across runs.

A single run represents approximately one year of a bird's life, compressed into 20 minutes. Time is continuous — there is no pause between phases. Environmental cues (light quality, foliage color, snow, temperature shimmer) signal seasonal transitions. The bird's body grows visibly across the run: fledgling to juvenile to adult. There is no clock on screen. This is one game you cannot speedrun.

Run Structure

Fledgling → Juvenile (mins 0–5) → Spring. First flights. High predation risk. Learning foraging. All core mechanics introduced through survival pressure, not tutorial.

Adult (mins 5–14) → Summer through Fall. Full mobility. Foraging, territorial defense, mate-finding. Seasonal threat escalation.

Winter Survival (mins 14–17) → High stamina drain, low food, low predators. Pure resource management.

Breeding Season (mins 17–20) → Spring 2. Mate-finding, nest-building, egg-tending, nest defense. Red Light / Green Light final sequence. Win condition: fledge the nest.

Win condition: Successfully fledge at least one chick from the breeding nest. Partial success (some chicks survive) counts. Zero survivors = run failure even if the bird is alive.

Loss conditions: Bird death (predation, starvation, collision, poison, hunter). Nest total loss during breeding phase. Stamina reaching zero and remaining there (starvation).

Death Card — Design Resolved

Death is permanent and instant. The death animation is brief, expressive, and slightly absurd. Then: the screen fades to black and white. A Sanrio-style rounded font displays the cause of death as an ecological punchline:

  • Raptor kill → [SPECIES] LUNCH
  • Cat kill → CAT LUNCH
  • Starvation → VULTURE FOOD
  • Human threat (poison, hunter) → no message. no sound. fade to black and white. silence. First occurrence only. Subsequent human deaths show the message. The silence is the point.

Then: survival time displayed, cause illustrated, back to species select. The tone is Dark Souls filtered through Hello Kitty.

03 —

Controls

The game uses four face buttons plus dual analog stick. Button assignments are fully remappable. Buttons map to the bird's body — A is feet, B is beak, X is wings, Y is social. This is the entire vocabulary. A player who understands the bird understands the controls.

Stillness = no button input + no stick input. The bird breathes. Awareness accumulates.
Contextual Priority Rules

Threat suppresses Y entirely. When the threat border is active, Y is disabled — no singing, no flock recruitment, no territorial display. You cannot advertise when a predator is present. This is ecologically accurate and resolves the most dangerous input collision: a player attempting to sing while a hawk is circling will find the button inert. The border is the signal.

Beak tiebreaker: If B has multiple valid contexts simultaneously (near food AND near a threat), threat alarm always fires. Eating requires a clear environment. Feeding chicks at the nest overrides alarm if the threat border is amber or below — the parent keeps feeding until red.

Context must be unambiguous before the game enables an action. Environmental cues (faint food glow, perch shimmer) communicate available actions without HUD prompts. First-time contexts show a brief button label (1–2 seconds, never repeats).

04 —

Life Cycle & Seasons

Twenty minutes maps to twelve months plus a second spring breeding window. Each season has a distinct visual identity, threat profile, food availability, and stamina economy. Season transitions are marked by title cards — the only full-screen UI moment in the game outside the death card. Where the death card drains color, the season card floods it: bloom, saturation spike, glowing script with the season's name. Same visual language, opposite valence. You made it. The world is beautiful.

Season title cards are the game's checkpoint system. On the card, the game saves — but only quit-and-resume is supported. Death remains permanent. The card does not say: try again from here. It says: consider taking a break.

What the checkpoint saves: Accumulated advantages and disadvantages carry forward. Overharvested food patches stay depleted. NPC hostility persists — the crow you alarmed in Spring is still agitated in Fall. A farmer whose feeder you raided has moved the feeder. The landscape remembers. Players who forage sustainably and avoid unnecessary conflict carry a cleaner world into Winter. Players who burned everything down will feel it.

There is no run clock on screen. Environmental cues signal seasonal change — light temperature, foliage, weather, ambient sound. First-time players will not know Winter is coming. That first Winter death is part of the game's reputation. It is not softened.

Core Mechanics

Stamina

Stamina is the game's primary resource. It drains continuously at a rate determined by season and activity. It refills only by eating. The bird cannot sprint, flap aggressively, sing, or defend without stamina cost. At zero stamina, the bird enters a distress state: sluggish movement, no vocalization, increased predator attraction. Prolonged zero stamina = death.

Stillness is the lowest drain state — the bird is not using energy, but it is also not eating. Stillness is never free; it is just cheaper than moving. The player must constantly weigh: does the environment justify burning stamina to forage, or is there a threat that makes stillness the better bet?

Awareness, Stillness & Collective Intelligence

The bird has an Awareness stat, invisible to the player but governing predator detection range and stealth. Awareness increases while the bird is still and decreases while moving. A still bird on a high perch with good sightlines has dramatically better threat detection than a bird hopping through undergrowth. Movement — especially rapid movement — generates visual and auditory signatures that attract threats.

The optimal solo play pattern is: move → perch → still → assess → move. Players who skip the perch-and-assess step die more. There is no awareness meter on screen. Players learn to feel it through predator behavior — are they scanning? Agitated? The read is the skill.

Collective Awareness — Gregarious Classes

Gregarious birds gain awareness faster and further when in a flock. Tap Y to recruit nearby NPC birds (contact call). Once a flock assembles, the collective awareness radius expands — each bird is a sensor node. Flock geometry determines both the speed and bounds of awareness gain: a tight cluster gives fast awareness in a small radius; a spread flock gives slower but much wider coverage. Players who consciously position themselves within a flock are playing a different, deeper game.

Solitary Awareness — Solo Classes

Solitary classes — Herons, Accipiters, Buteos, Mimids — cannot recruit flocks and gain no collective awareness bonus. In compensation, they have a sharper, wider baseline awareness and gain more from stillness than any gregarious class. A perfectly still heron at water's edge has detection range no flock can match. A Cooper's Hawk motionless in a forest gap is functionally invisible and sees everything.

This makes solitary vs. gregarious a genuine strategic axis. Gregarious birds survive through shared intelligence and social buffering. Solitary birds survive through individual mastery of stillness. Both paths lead to the same destination; neither is easier.

V2 Note — Owls

Owls will be the solitary awareness ceiling: nocturnal masters, the apex of stillness-as-mechanic, the thing everything else fears. Their absence in V1 is intentional. They need to be earned by the player base first.

Mate Finding (Adult Phase)

To complete a run, the bird must acquire a mate before the breeding window opens. Mate-finding requires:

  1. Reaching a visible, elevated perch (detected automatically when in range)
  2. Holding B to sing — stamina drains at 1.5× while singing
  3. Maintaining the song for long enough that a mate responds (duration scales with species — warblers take longest)
  4. Not dying while doing it

If the mate is killed during the breeding window, a second mate can be found — but the window is short and the stamina cost is high. It's a rescue, not a mulligan. Chicks already hatched remain in the nest; the clock does not stop.

Singing makes the bird highly visible. The threat border becomes more sensitive while singing. This is the game's central tension made explicit: to breed, you must advertise. To advertise, you must accept exposure. There is no safe place to sing.

Nest Defense (Red Light / Green Light)

The final sequence begins once eggs are laid. The bird must:

  • Green light: Leave the nest to forage. Return to feed chicks (B near nest). Each chick has a visible fledgling meter — a small fill bar that jumps with each successful feeding and drains slowly between feeds.
  • Red light: Predator detected. Return immediately. Go still. Border pulses. Do not call, do not move. Feeding is suppressed while border is red.
  • Fail state: Predator reaches undefended nest, or parent is killed during defense.

Score and camera: As the breeding sequence begins, the score layers in a heartbeat — steady, present, quietly insistent. It does not accelerate with threat; it simply stays. Each chick meter that fills triggers a brief musical swell. Each chick that fledges is a discrete win moment: the camera holds on the nest for a beat, then releases.

Camera and scale: During the breeding sequence, the camera always keeps the nest in frame — even at distance. At Very Large bird scales, a parent far out foraging will see the nest as a tiny point in the landscape. The camera makes the player feel the distance. The heartbeat makes them feel the stakes. This is intentional: the game wants the player to feel very small and very responsible at the same time.

Chicks fledge one by one over the final 2–3 minutes. Fledge all chicks = perfect run. Fledge at least one = successful run. Zero survivors = run failure even if the parent is alive.

Bird Classes & Species

There are 12 bird classes in V1. Each class has a unique mechanic, a dedicated biome, and a species unlock tree. Classes are unlocked progressively; species within a class unlock via milestones. All species within a class share the same core mechanic but have distinct stat profiles and visual identities.

Ecological accuracy governs biome assignment. Where multiple species in a class occupy different biomes (e.g., ravens vs. crows vs. jays within corvids), scale and world-size adjust per species while the biome itself is consistent with real range.

Corvid Tool Use — Full Object Reference

Corvids are the only class with environmental tool use. The mechanic is consistent: approach an interactable object → hold B → a God of War-style button sequence appears → match it → reward. Sequence complexity scales inversely with the number of corvid NPCs present. More crows = simpler pattern = bigger chaos energy. Solo corvid sequences are hardest.

Corvid NPCs are ambient in all corvid biomes. They become recruitable by cawing (tap B with no other context active). Nearby corvids get curious and follow. Some interactions — dumpsters, car nutcracking — only unlock once at least one NPC has joined.

Farmer Ecology — Seasonal Reference

The farmer is one of the richest environmental systems in the game for corvids and adjacent species. Farmers are not simply threats — they are a seasonal risk/reward landscape. Learning the farmer's schedule is the corvid equivalent of learning a patrol route.

Spray = Silent Poison

Crop-edge insects near a recently sprayed field are a food source with no visible warning. No threat border. Death is delayed 3–5 seconds after eating — enough to register the cause. First occurrence: silence, no message. Same mechanic as hunter poison. This is not an accident — it is the same human indifference, different context.

Each class has a primary biome. Biomes are procedurally generated per run using a seed. Generation guarantees: sufficient food density per season, at least one viable nest site, at least one high-awareness perch location, and class-appropriate threat population. No two runs produce identical layouts.

World Scale System

World size and camera distance scale with the physical size of the bird. The asset library, interaction logic, and mesh vocabulary are identical across all species — a dumpster is a dumpster, a tree is a tree. What changes is how much world exists, how far the camera pulls back, and how sparsely objects are distributed across the generated terrain. Interaction density remains constant: every bird encounters roughly the same number of food sources, threats, and interactables per unit of stamina-travel. The raven's world is simply much larger.

This makes species unlock sequencing meaningful beyond stat progression. Playing a warbler after a raven feels genuinely claustrophobic. Playing a raven after a sparrow feels like the world has opened up. The same biome assets read completely differently at each scale — a suburban backyard that feels enormous to a warbler is a glance-and-pass to a crow.

Mesh density reduction at large scales: At Large and Very Large tiers, procedural generation reduces fine-detail mesh density (ground cover, undergrowth, small debris) to maintain performance. Landmark objects — trees, structures, water, interactables — remain consistent. The world reads more spare at raven scale, which is also ecologically correct: ravens navigate by landmark, not by foraging through undergrowth.

Technical Challenge — Flagged

A raven biome at 30× sparrow scale with consistent interaction density requires dynamic terrain and asset streaming — static scene loading is not viable at this scale differential. This is an architectural decision affecting the entire generation pipeline and should be confirmed before production begins on any Large-tier species. We are confident this is solvable; we flag it so it is scoped correctly from day one.

Threat Viability by Size Tier

Predator threat logic does not change between species — patrol routes, detection radii, and attack behaviors are constant. What changes is ecological viability: a sharp-shinned hawk does not hunt ravens. A domestic cat is not a credible threat to a great blue heron. The threat border simply does not fire for predators that are non-viable at a given size tier. Players moving up the size ladder will notice familiar threats going quiet — and new ones appearing.

Design Note — Threat Asymmetry

The absence of threat border for human threats (hunters, poison, lead) is intentional and should not be softened. These threats exist to teach ecological reality: for corvids and vultures, human activity is the most lethal threat precisely because it arrives without warning. The first death to poison should feel unfair. The second death to poison is the player's fault. This is the game's argument.

09 —

Progression & Unlocks

Nothing carries between runs except unlocks and the season checkpoint state. No stat bonuses, no persistent items, no remembered map layouts. The only durable advantage is knowing how the world works. Unlocks are milestone-gated and tracked across all runs. Every player begins as the American Robin. All other species and classes branch from that root.

Design principle: Bad deaths are progression moments. Dying to poison unlocks vultures. Dying to a window strike unlocks pigeons. A first human-threat death is silent and jarring — and unlocks corvids. The unlock tree is structured so that a curious player naturally branches outward, and a skilled player eventually reaches everything.

On Warblers

There are over 50 warbler species in North America. The game will never contain all of them and makes no apology for this. Warbler unlocks are intentionally opaque, occasionally absurd, and community-discovered. Some involve map seeds. Some involve specific undocumented behaviors. Some involve doing something the game never tells you is possible. This has been the experience of birding with warblers since birding began. The mystery is the point. Crow happy.

10 —

Camera & Presentation

The camera is third-person follow with full right-stick control. The player should always be able to look around independently of movement — this is core to the awareness mechanic. A bird that can look without moving is a safer bird.

The camera distance adjusts contextually: tighter during ground foraging and nest sequences, pulled back during flight. There should be a "flight awe" camera mode that kicks in when the bird achieves significant altitude or speed — a slight slow-motion + camera drift effect that communicates the beauty of the moment without interrupting play. This is the game saying: look at this.

Visual Direction

Bright, saturated, slightly stylized — not photorealistic, not cartoon. Think the color palette of a quality field guide illustration brought into 3D. Environments should be rich with detail but readable: the player should always be able to identify food, threat, and shelter at a glance through color language.

  • Food: Warm amber glow at close range (subtle, not UI)
  • Nest sites: Soft green shimmer
  • Safe perches: No marker — players must judge visually
  • Predators: No marker — threat border only
  • Seasons: Communicated through light temperature, foliage, weather, ambient sound

Starvation is communicated through progressive desaturation of the entire image — the world literally loses color as the bird weakens. Full-color world = healthy bird. Sepia world = feed immediately.

HUD

Near-zero. The only persistent screen elements are:

  • Threat border vignette (color + pulse rate)
  • World desaturation (stamina proxy)
  • Fledgling meters — visible only during breeding sequence, one small fill bar per chick, nest-adjacent only
  • Contextual button prompts — appear for 1–2 seconds on first encounter only; never repeat
  • Season title cards — full-screen bloom moment at each seasonal transition; the only deliberate UI interruption

No stamina bar, no health bar, no minimap, no waypoints, no timers, no achievement popups — except [SHINY OBJECT].

11 —

Audio Direction

Audio carries enormous narrative and mechanical weight given the minimal HUD. The soundscape must be rich, ecologically accurate, and tactically legible. Players should learn to read threats by sound before they read them visually.

  • Alarm calls from other species are the earliest threat warning — before the border activates. A distant chickadee alarm call means something is moving in that direction.
  • The bird's own vocalizations are species-accurate recordings or high-quality recreations. Song complexity is audibly meaningful for thrushes and mimids.
  • Seasonal ambient layers shift dynamically: spring insects and frogs, summer heat buzz, fall wind and leaf rustle, winter silence.
  • Stamina distress is signaled by a subtle change in the bird's breathing/wing sounds — a shortness that mirrors the visual desaturation.
  • Death sounds are brief and non-graphic. A small, surprised beat. Then silence. Then the death card.
  • Nest fledge is the loudest, most joyful moment in the game. Each chick that leaves the nest triggers a brief musical swell.
Music Direction

Score should be dynamic and season-responsive, not looping background music. Sparse in winter (solo instrument, long silences), full and layered in spring. Tempo tracks threat level subtly — not obviously, not mechanically. The Katamari influence lives here: the music should be playful and strange and occasionally overwhelming in the best possible way.

12 —

Technical Notes

Engine

Unreal Engine. Target platforms: PC (Steam), PS5, Xbox Series X/S, Nintendo Switch. Switch performance budget is the binding constraint for all rendering decisions.

Procedural Generation

Per-run seed generation for biome layout. Required guarantees per generated map: food source density within survivable stamina range of spawn point, at least one high-sightline perch within 30 seconds of spawn, nest site options (minimum 2) within adult territory range, appropriate predator population density per season. Generation must not create maps where the win condition is mechanically impossible.

Scale & World Size

World size scales with bird size across five tiers (Tiny → Very Large), from 1× baseline for warblers to 25–30× for vultures and condors. Camera distance, object density, and terrain generation density all scale accordingly. Asset meshes and interaction logic are shared across all tiers. See §07 (World Scale System) for the full specification and technical challenge flag.

Save State

No mid-run saves. Runs must be completed or abandoned. The milestone/unlock database persists between sessions. No cloud save requirement for V1 but architecture should anticipate it.

Accessibility

Fully remappable controls. Colorblind modes must address the threat border (color + pattern/pulse rate should both carry the signal, not color alone). Subtitle support for any voiced UI. Adjustable camera sensitivity.
